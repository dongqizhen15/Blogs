Atmospheric rivers: a mini-review

  • Abstract:
  • This article summarized the main characteristics of ARs. The main advantage of the AR conceptual model is its ability to link intense atmospheric transport of moisture across the midlatitudes with heavy precipitation events. It is simple and easy to visualize, allowing simple computations on AR positions, changes, and regional and global contributions of ARs to the hydrological cycle.

  • What an AR is and its structure
  • An AR is long (about 2000 km), narrow (about 300–500 km wide) bands of enhanced water vapor flux (>90% of the total mid-latitude vertically integrated water vapor flux) across the mid-latitudes toward higher latitudes. It’s also called as “tropical moisture exports” or “moisture conveyor belts”. ARs also have colloquial names, such as “Hawaiian fire hose” or “Pineapple Express”.
    Some of the properties are: (1) a concentrated band of enhanced low-level specific humidity; (2) a pre-cold-frontal low level jet due to the temperature gradient across the cold front; (3) the vertical distribution of the equivalent potential temperature shows moist-neutral stratification of the AR, low-level potential instability on the cold side of the front, and an area of subsidence at low levels ahead of the AR. The moisture in an AR has two origins: (1) local moisture convergence along the cold front of the extratropical cyclone; (2) direct poleward transport of tropical moisture. The three different precipitation regimes observed in an AR event are cold, warm and mixed.
  • METHODS TO IDENTIFY ATMOSPHERIC RIVERS
  • The methods to identify them are: (i) by using Integrated Water Vapor (IWV) from satellite measurements , reanalyses or models and then applying criteria on the IWV (in terms of IWV amount, length and width); (ii) methods that calculate the vertically integrated horizontal water vapor transport (IVT) between 1000 hPa and 300 hPa from atmospheric reanalyses or models and then employ thresholds on IVT to define an AR. (iii) By the identification of hydrological extremes to assess the atmospheric state to determine whether an AR caused the extremes.

  • MODELS TO PREDICT ARs and ARs in a changing climate
  • ARs can be represented and predicted by numerical weather prediction (NWP) models. The problem is it can have large errors such as the timing and position of landfall.
    ARs’ occurrence probability may be influenced by large-scale ocean-atmosphere dynamics (OAD) such as NAO or ENSO. Several examples were also presented showing negative or positive relations between ARs activity and OAD.
    It is important to know possible changes in the frequency and intensity of ARs in future. For example, all the evidence for California points toward an enhanced flood risk from ARs.
