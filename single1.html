Atmospheric moisture transport(AMS) VS precipitation across the Tibetan Plateau: A mini-review and current challenges
  • Abstract:

    • This article mainly discussed the recent progresses of ARs across TP and the research challenges and prospects.

  • Full blog:

    • AMS and associated circulation patterns in a changing climate


    Filamentary structure is a common feature for the atmospheric moisture transport. There are four primary climate systems regulating the moisture transport to the TP, the Indian monsoon system, the mid-latitude Westerlies, the East Asian monsoon system and the local moisture recycling. In summer, the TP serves as a strong “dynamic pump” and continuously attracts moist air from the surrounding oceans through deep canyons. The process of water vapor transport into the TP was explained. The summer precipitation was influenced by thermal effects in internal TP, and also the atmospheric moisture transport from outside.


    Several approaches to identify AMS


    The distribution and movement of water vapor can be quantified using satellite retrievals and/or ground-based observations. There are four approaches: IWV, IVT, TMEs, and water isotopes. The identification is through a basis of a zonal threshold or a zonal and meridional threshold, , some features like time step are considered too.


    IWV is considered as a proxy to characterize narrow features describing most of the instantaneous meridional water vapor transport at mid-latitudes. But it is hard to quantify. Not all the water vapor is precipitable, so further definition is needed in TP.


    The Lagrangian method is also developed to quantify the contribution of the atmospheric moisture transport to the precipitation. The identification process of TMEs (tropical moisture transports ) includes three steps in which the threshold is defined by the researcher. There are some disadvantages. Many trajectory analysis tools are FLEXTRA , HYSPLIT and so on.
